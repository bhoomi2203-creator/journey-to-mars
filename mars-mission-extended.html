Phase 02 · Deep Space Transit
HOHMANN TRANSFER ORBIT
THE MOST FUEL-EFFICIENT PATH BETWEEN TWO PLANETS

HOW IT WORKS

A Hohmann transfer is an orbital maneuver using two engine burns to move a spacecraft from one circular orbit to another. First proposed by Walter Hohmann in 1925, it remains the gold standard for interplanetary travel.

The spacecraft fires its engine to enter an elliptical transfer orbit — the semi-major axis of this ellipse exactly bridges the two planetary orbits. A second burn at the far end circularizes the orbit at the destination.

EARTH → MARS TRANSFER

▷ Burn 1: +2.9 km/s from LEO to leave Earth
▷ Transfer time: ~259 days (7+ months)
▷ Burn 2: +0.9 km/s to enter Mars orbit
▷ Launch window: Every 26 months
▷ Transfer orbit aphelion: 1.524 AU
▷ Eccentricity: ~0.208

BEYOND OUR SOLAR SYSTEM

Our mission passes through a tiny corner of the Milky Way. The universe is incomprehensibly vast — yet humanity dares reach for the nearest star's companion planet.

☀️
Our Solar System
0 Light Years · Home
8 planets, 1 star, and billions of smaller bodies occupying ~0.0000316 light years. Our cradle — and first prison to escape. Mars is the stepping stone.
🌌
Milky Way Galaxy
100,000 Light Years Wide
Our home galaxy — a barred spiral containing 200–400 billion stars. We orbit ~26,000 light years from the galactic center, making one full revolution every 225 million years.
🔭
Andromeda Galaxy
2.537 Million Light Years
Our nearest large galactic neighbor. On a collision course with the Milky Way — impact expected in ~4.5 billion years. Contains ~1 trillion stars and likely hundreds of billions of planets.
💫
Proxima Centauri b
4.24 Light Years · Nearest Exoplanet
Closest known exoplanet in the habitable zone of Proxima Centauri. At current rocket speeds, a journey would take ~75,000 years. Future ion drives may cut this to centuries.
🌠
Observable Universe
93 Billion Light Years Diameter
The observable universe contains approximately 2 trillion galaxies, each with hundreds of billions of stars. Humanity has left Earth's surface. Mars is the next permanent outpost.
🪐
Voyager 1 — Interstellar
23.7 Billion km · Farthest Man-Made Object
Launched in 1977, Voyager 1 entered interstellar space in 2012. Traveling at 17 km/s, it will take 40,000 years to reach the next star system — a humbling testament to cosmic scale.
Phase 03 · Mars Arrival

MARS ARRIVAL

After 259 days in deep space, the first glimpse of Mars fills every porthole. Rust-red and ancient, it grows from a point of light to a world. Atmospheric entry begins.

ENTRY, DESCENT & LANDING

01
PHASE · ATMOSPHERIC ENTRY
Hitting the Atmosphere
The spacecraft slams into Mars' thin atmosphere at Mach 24 — 24 times the speed of sound. Atmospheric friction generates plasma temperatures of 1,627°C around the heat shield.
ALTITUDE: 125 KM · VELOCITY: 5,600 M/S
02
PHASE · PEAK HEATING
The 7 Minutes of Terror
Blackout period — no communication possible through plasma sheath. The spacecraft is alone. Only physics and engineering matter now. 7 minutes from entry to landing.
ALTITUDE: 11 KM · VELOCITY: 400 M/S
03
PHASE · PARACHUTE DEPLOY
Supersonic Drag
A 21-meter supersonic parachute deploys at Mach 2.2, generating 30,000 N of drag force. The heat shield drops away. Radar begins scanning for landing site hazards.
ALTITUDE: 1.6 KM · VELOCITY: 80 M/S
04
PHASE · POWERED DESCENT
Retro-Rockets Fire
Eight retro-rockets fire simultaneously. 3,600 N of thrust guides the spacecraft to the pre-selected landing zone. Terrain-relative navigation makes real-time corrections.
ALTITUDE: 20 M · VELOCITY: 2 M/S
05
PHASE · TOUCHDOWN ✓
Landing Confirmed
Landing legs absorb impact at Jezero Crater floor. Signal takes 11 minutes to reach Earth. Mission Control erupts. Humanity has a new outpost. The exploration begins.

Space Mission Knowledge Base
🛸
Ion Propulsion
Future: 90-day Mars transit
Ion drives use electric fields to accelerate xenon ions to extreme velocities. Low thrust but highly efficient — future missions could use solar or nuclear-powered ion engines to cut transit time dramatically.
☢️
Nuclear Propulsion
NERVA/NTP Programs
Nuclear thermal propulsion heats hydrogen propellant using a reactor to produce ~900 seconds of specific impulse — double chemical rockets. Could cut Earth-Mars transit to 45 days with nuclear pulse drives.
🌡️
ISRU — In-Situ Resources
Sabatier Reaction
Mars' CO₂ atmosphere can be converted to methane and oxygen using the Sabatier reaction with hydrogen. MOXIE on Perseverance already produces oxygen on Mars, validating this for future propellant plants.
🧬
Radiation Protection
500 mSv/year on surface
Mars lacks a global magnetic field. Colonists will need underground habitats, polyethylene shielding, or pharmaceutical radiation protection. Regolith walls 2m thick reduce dose to Earth-level background.
🌱
Terraforming
10,000+ year project
Warming Mars requires releasing its polar CO₂ ice. Orbital mirrors, engineered bacteria, and fluorocarbon gas factories could create a runaway greenhouse effect — eventually yielding liquid surface water.
🔋
Solar Power on Mars
590 W/m² at perihelion
Mars receives ~43% of Earth's solar irradiance, but dust storms can reduce this to near zero for months. Early colonies will rely on nuclear fission microreactors (Kilopower) as primary power.
